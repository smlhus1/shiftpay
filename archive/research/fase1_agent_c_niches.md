# Research: Underserved Niche Groups - Real Problems Tech Ignores

> Researched: 2026-02-13 | Sources consulted: 40+ | Confidence: High

## TL;DR

The most acute, underserved pain points cluster around **family caregivers coordinating elder care**, **small volunteer associations drowning in admin**, and **digitally excluded elderly in Norway**. These groups experience daily frustration, have few purpose-built tools, and represent genuine human suffering that tech systematically ignores because the users lack purchasing power or technical literacy. For a 5-day hackathon MVP, **caregiver coordination** and **small association management** offer the best combination of real pain, buildable scope, and demo-ability.

---

## Problem 1: Family Caregiver Coordination Chaos
**Pain level:** 9/10
**Frequency:** Daily
**Underserved group:** Adult children (often siblings) caring for aging/sick parents
**Description:** When an elderly parent needs care, siblings scramble to coordinate who visited, what medications were given, which doctor said what, and who's handling next week. Communication happens across WhatsApp, phone calls, email, and sticky notes. Missed medications, duplicated efforts, and sibling conflicts are constant. One sibling typically bears 80% of the load while others remain uninformed.
**Source:** CareScout, Caring Village, ElderLife Financial, NYU CareMobi research, multiple caregiver app reviews (2024-2025)
**Why tech ignores them:** Caregivers are overwhelmed, time-poor, and not a "sexy" market. They're unlikely to comparison-shop for tools. Revenue per user is low. The care recipient (elderly person) adds complexity with low tech literacy.
**Current solutions:** Caring Village, Lotsa Helping Hands, CareZone, Medisafe exist but are US-focused, require significant onboarding, and most families still resort to group chats. weCare exists in Norway (Stiftelsen Dam-funded) but appears small-scale. None nail the "dead simple daily check-in" that exhausted caregivers need.
**Norwegian angle:** EXTREMELY relevant. Norway launched "parorendeavtaler" (caregiver agreements) in 2025. DIGGPa digital guide launched for professional caregiver work. But nothing simple exists for family caregivers. Helsenorge covers patient data but not family coordination. An estimated 800,000 Norwegians provide unpaid care.

## Problem 2: Small Association/Club Admin Nightmare
**Pain level:** 8/10
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Underserved group:** Volunteer leaders of small sports clubs, cultural associations, neighborhood groups (50-500 members)
**Description:** The volunteer treasurer/secretary of a small club juggles member lists in Excel, dues collection via Vipps with no tracking, event coordination in Facebook groups, meeting minutes in Word docs, and financial reporting on paper. When the treasurer quits, all institutional knowledge walks out the door. 30% of nonprofits still manage volunteers with spreadsheets, costing 10-15 hours/week in admin overhead.
**Source:** VolunteerHub (Oct 2025), MemberJungle, Frivillighet Norge, Rubic.no, Spond Club, multiple nonprofit management surveys
**Why tech ignores them:** Zero budget. Volunteer leaders rotate every 1-2 years (no long-term customer). Too small for enterprise tools, too complex for simple apps. The Norwegian "forening" model (50,000+ local chapters) is too niche for international players.
**Current solutions:** Spond (good for sports scheduling, weak on financials), Rubic (Norwegian, more enterprise-oriented), spreadsheets, Facebook groups. Most solutions cost $50-200/month which tiny clubs can't justify. The gap is a FREE, dead-simple all-in-one for clubs under 200 members.
**Norwegian angle:** Norway has over 50,000 local voluntary organizations (Frivillighet Norge). The "dugnad" culture means millions participate. VAT compensation for voluntary orgs is a hot political topic (500M NOK/year in VAT). Managing these orgs is almost exclusively done by unpaid volunteers with zero training.

## Problem 3: Elderly Digital Exclusion in Norway
**Pain level:** 8/10
**Frequency:** Daily
**Underserved group:** Norwegians 65+, especially 75+, who struggle with mandatory digital services
**Description:** 850,000 Norwegians are "digitally vulnerable" according to Digitaliseringsdirektoratet. 1 in 4 elderly fear falling outside society because they can't use digital public services. They struggle with banking (BankID), health portals (Helsenorge), public transport tickets, and government services. 1 in 4 elderly have experienced digital fraud/scams. When physical bank branches and service offices close, they have nowhere to turn.
**Source:** Norges institusjon for menneskerettigheter (NIM, Oct 2025), NRK ("600,000 sliter digitalt"), Sykepleien (Oct 2025), forskning.no, UU-tilsynet
**Why tech ignores them:** They literally cannot use the products being built. They don't download apps, don't leave app store reviews, don't tweet complaints. Their children/grandchildren help ad-hoc. No revenue model. "Just wait for the problem to solve itself" attitude from industry.
**Current solutions:** Seniornett (government-funded, 5M NOK in 2025) provides courses but not real-time help. Libraries offer some assistance. Family members provide informal help but it's exhausting for both sides.
**Norwegian angle:** This IS a Norwegian story. Norway is one of the world's most digitized societies, which paradoxically creates deeper exclusion. Services that used to be accessible in person are now digital-only. Government is allocating money (Seniornett) but throwing courses at people who need ongoing, in-the-moment support.

## Problem 4: Immigrant Bureaucracy Navigation in Norway
**Pain level:** 8/10
**Frequency:** Weekly (intense bursts during first 6-12 months)
**Underserved group:** Newly arrived immigrants/refugees in Norway
**Description:** Navigating UDI, IMDi, NAV, and kommune simultaneously is bewildering. Each agency has different portals, different requirements, different contact methods. You need BankID to do almost anything, but getting BankID requires a bank account, which requires a residence permit, which requires... a circular dependency maze. The introduction programme varies wildly between municipalities. Language barriers compound everything.
**Source:** UDI, IMDi, Nordic Council (Nord2025-038), forskning.no (2025), regjeringen.no integration reports
**Why tech ignores them:** Low purchasing power. Language diversity makes localization expensive. Bureaucratic processes differ per municipality. Small, fragmented market per country.
**Current solutions:** UDI website (information-heavy, not action-oriented), FindHello (US-focused), MigApp (IOM, generic). No Norway-specific "settlement checklist" app exists. IMDi has information but not an interactive tool. Volunteers and social workers fill the gap manually.
**Norwegian angle:** VERY relevant. 80,000 Ukrainians arrived 2022-2025. Introduction programme varies by kommune. Healthcare system frustration is documented (forskning.no: immigrants leave Norway partly due to healthcare dissatisfaction). New A2 language requirement from Sept 2025 adds pressure.

## Problem 5: Solo Tradesperson Admin Overload
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** Daily
**Underserved group:** One-person plumbing, electrical, carpentry businesses (enkeltmannsforetak)
**Description:** Solo tradespeople spend 2.5 hours per week just on quoting and invoicing. They type quotes in Word, track jobs on paper, chase payments manually, and forget to track certifications/licenses. They lose customers because they can't respond fast enough with professional quotes. Many still use paper receipts thrown in a shoebox for their accountant.
**Source:** Workever (2025), BuildOps, Checkatrade, Debet Faktura, multiple trade software reviews
**Why tech ignores them:** These users have low tech comfort, won't pay premium SaaS prices, and work with their hands all day (limited screen time). International solutions (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro) are overkill and US-focused. Norwegian options (Debet, Fakto) exist but aren't mobile-first for field workers.
**Current solutions:** Tradify, Workever, Joist (international, English-only). Norwegian: Fakto.no (free basic invoicing), Debet Faktura, Tripletex (too complex). Gap: a dead-simple mobile app for Norwegian sole traders that handles quote-to-invoice-to-payment in 60 seconds, in Norwegian, with Vipps integration.
**Norwegian angle:** Highly relevant. Norway has ~100,000+ enkeltmannsforetak in trades. Norwegian invoicing has specific legal requirements (organisasjonsnummer, MVA). Existing solutions are either too enterprise or too basic.

## Problem 6: Volunteer Treasurer's Accounting Hell
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Underserved group:** Volunteer treasurers of small clubs, parent groups (FAU), hobby associations
**Description:** A parent who volunteers as treasurer for their kid's football club suddenly needs to track membership dues, dugnad income, tournament expenses, equipment purchases, and produce an annual report for the general assembly. They have no accounting training, use a personal Excel template from 2015, and dread tax season. When they hand off to the next treasurer, the transition is chaos. Chasing late payments alone eats all available volunteer time.
**Source:** MoneyMinder, BluAvocado, Clubforce, FastPay, AccountingForEveryone
**Why tech ignores them:** Near-zero willingness to pay. Users rotate annually. No IT department to manage tools. Too small for QuickBooks, too complex for a spreadsheet. The "market" is fragmented across thousands of tiny orgs.
**Current solutions:** Spreadsheets (majority), MoneyMinder (English, $179/year), Clubtreasurer.com (UK-focused). Norwegian: Rubic has some financial features but targets larger orgs. Most clubs use Vipps for dues but track nothing systematically.
**Norwegian angle:** FAU (school parent groups), idrettslag (sports clubs), and velforeninger (neighborhood associations) are cornerstone Norwegian institutions. Every school has an FAU with a volunteer treasurer. Vipps is ubiquitous for payments but provides no accounting/reporting for organizations.

## Problem 7: Student Housing Crisis Navigation
**Pain level:** 8/10
**Frequency:** Seasonal (August peak) but persistent stress
**Underserved group:** Students seeking affordable housing, especially in Oslo, Bergen, Trondheim
**Description:** 6,000+ students on waiting lists for SiO housing in Oslo (2025). A full-time student on student loans alone cannot afford any private market housing in Oslo. Students frantically refresh Finn.no, Hybel.no, and Facebook groups, losing apartments within minutes. Scams are rampant on informal channels. International students face additional barriers (no Norwegian references, unfamiliar rental laws).
**Source:** VG, Universitas, NRK, SiO, Studenttorget
**Why tech ignores them:** Finn.no dominates the Norwegian housing market. Students have no money (CAC impossible). The problem is fundamentally a supply problem, not a tech problem. However, the discovery/matching/alert process is broken.
**Current solutions:** SiO waiting list (limited supply), Finn.no (expensive ads, no student focus), Hybel.no, Facebook groups (scam-ridden). No tool aggregates all sources, filters for student budgets, sends instant alerts, and provides scam detection.
**Norwegian angle:** Extremely Norwegian. 6,000 in queue for Oslo alone. International allocation controversy (80% of first batch to international students). Government building 5,000 units but still insufficient.

## Problem 8: Rural Community Service Coordination
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Weekly
**Underserved group:** Residents of small Norwegian communities (bygder) losing services
**Description:** Bank branches, post offices, grocery stores, and health services are disappearing from rural communities. Residents need to coordinate rides to the nearest town, share resources, and communicate about local services. The digital divide hits harder here: worse broadband, older demographics, fewer tech-savvy neighbors.
**Source:** The Grand Bargain Project, NPR (rural digital divide), Norwegian rural policy discussions
**Why tech ignores them:** Tiny addressable market per community. Infrastructure (broadband) limitations. Older demographics with lower tech adoption. No venture-scale returns.
**Current solutions:** Local Facebook groups (main coordination tool), WhatsApp groups, community bulletin boards. Nextdoor exists but is US-centric. No purpose-built "rural community coordination" tool.
**Norwegian angle:** Relevant but less acute than urban issues. "Distriktspolitikk" is a hot political topic. Some municipalities are experimenting with digital village hubs.

## Problem 9: Elderly Family Tech Support Burden
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** Weekly (multiple calls/visits)
**Underserved group:** Adult children providing ad-hoc tech support to elderly parents
**Description:** Every time BankID stops working, Helsenorge changes layout, or a scam email arrives, elderly parents call their adult children for help. This creates invisible labor: driving over on weekends to fix the TV box, spending 45 minutes on the phone explaining how to log in, or discovering that a parent accidentally subscribed to something. NRK reported 600,000 Norwegians regularly need help from grandchildren for digital tasks.
**Source:** NRK (2025), NIM, Seniornett, forskning.no
**Why tech ignores them:** The "customer" (elderly person) can't articulate the problem or search for solutions. The "buyer" (adult child) doesn't realize this is a solvable problem -- they see it as "just life." No clear revenue model.
**Current solutions:** Family/grandchildren (100% of the support), Seniornett courses (preventive, not real-time), library drop-in help (limited hours). No "remote tech support dashboard for parents" exists that lets a child see what their parent is trying to do and guide them.
**Norwegian angle:** Very Norwegian. 600,000 people needing regular help (NRK). BankID as a chokepoint for everything digital. Digital-first government services create dependence on family support networks.

## Problem 10: Caregiver Medication & Appointment Tracking
**Pain level:** 8/10
**Frequency:** Daily
**Underserved group:** Family members managing complex medication regimens for elderly/chronically ill loved ones
**Description:** An elderly parent takes 8 different medications at different times, sees 3 specialists quarterly, and has changing dosages. The primary caregiver (often one daughter/son) tracks this in their head, on paper notes, or in phone reminders. When a home nurse visits, they ask "has mom taken her morning pills?" and nobody is sure. Errors in medication management cause 250,000+ hospitalizations annually in the US alone.
**Source:** Medisafe, CareZone, Happy To Help Caregiving, Connected Caregiver
**Why tech ignores them:** Complex medical compliance is scary territory for apps (liability concerns). The user (caregiver) is exhausted and has low tolerance for onboarding. Multi-user coordination adds technical complexity. Integrating with actual pharmacy/health systems is a regulatory nightmare.
**Current solutions:** Medisafe (good for individual use, weak on family coordination), CareZone (discontinued/merged), pill organizer boxes (physical). In Norway: Helsenorge shows prescriptions but doesn't coordinate family caregiving.
**Norwegian angle:** Helsenorge app shows medical info but is patient-facing, not caregiver-facing. Multidose (pre-packaged medication rolls) exists for home care patients but not for family-managed care. "Fullmakt" (power of attorney) for health data access is bureaucratic.

## Problem 11: Small Nonprofit Event Volunteer No-Shows
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** Per event (monthly for active orgs)
**Underserved group:** Small charity/community event organizers
**Description:** You organize a community event, 30 volunteers sign up on a spreadsheet. Day of the event: 12 show up. 5 people signed up for the same station, nobody signed up for parking. Reminders were sent by email (half went to spam). No-show rates of 30-50% are common. Organizations using automated systems report 40% reduction in no-shows.
**Source:** VolunteerHub (2025), SignUpGenius, Galaxy Digital, Nonprofit Learning Lab
**Why tech ignores them:** Small events don't generate enough revenue to attract SaaS companies. Volunteer coordinators are themselves volunteers -- they won't champion a paid tool purchase. The buying cycle is impossible (who approves the purchase in a volunteer org?).
**Current solutions:** SignUpGenius (free tier, US-focused), Spond (good for sports, less for community events), Google Sheets + email. In Norway: Spond dominates sports scheduling but many orgs use Facebook events.
**Norwegian angle:** Dugnadskultur. Every idrettslag, FAU, and velforening runs events that need volunteers. Spond is used widely but primarily for recurring practices, not ad-hoc events/dugnad scheduling.

## Problem 12: Construction Worker Weather-Dependent Planning
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Daily during outdoor season
**Underserved group:** Small construction crews, painters, roofers, landscapers
**Description:** Outdoor tradespeople check weather forecasts religiously but manually reschedule work when rain/wind/frost hits. Clients aren't notified consistently. Materials ordered for a specific day sit unused. Crews show up to a job site only to realize conditions make work impossible (or unsafe). No tool integrates weather forecasting with job scheduling and automatic client notification.
**Source:** Multiple farm/construction weather app reviews, trade scheduling software reviews
**Why tech ignores them:** Weather APIs exist but nobody has bridged the gap to trade scheduling. The target users don't search for "weather-integrated scheduling" -- they don't know it could exist. Small crew sizes make per-seat SaaS unattractive.
**Current solutions:** Yr.no (weather only), manual rescheduling via phone/SMS, calendar apps with no weather integration.
**Norwegian angle:** Norwegian weather is notoriously variable. "Yr.no" is the national weather obsession. Construction season is short (May-October for outdoor work). Weather delays cost the Norwegian construction industry billions annually.

## Problem 13: Student Finance Survival Mode
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** Daily
**Underserved group:** Students living on student loans + part-time income
**Description:** Norwegian students get ~12,500 NOK/month from Lanekassen. Rent in Oslo can be 8,000-10,000 NOK. They need to track every krone but generic budget apps (YNAB, Mint) aren't designed for the student reality: irregular part-time income, semester-based loan disbursements, food sharing with roommates, and the need to know "can I afford to go out this Friday?" rather than "are you on track for retirement?"
**Source:** College Raptor, YNAB, MTSU Financial Literacy Center, Norwegian student housing data
**Why tech ignores them:** Students have no money to pay for apps. They'll age out of the product in 3-5 years. Generic budget apps capture some of this market. Student-specific features are hard to monetize.
**Current solutions:** YNAB (free first year for students, but complex), Spenderlog (Norwegian, basic), bank apps with spending categories (Sbanken/DNB). No tool is specifically built around the Norwegian student loan cycle with Lanekassen integration.
**Norwegian angle:** Lanekassen loan/stipend structure is unique. Semester-based disbursements create boom-bust cycles. "Fadderuke" and social pressure to spend. Student welfare organizations (samskipnader) could be distribution partners.

## Problem 14: Immigrant Healthcare Navigation
**Pain level:** 7/10
**Frequency:** When sick/injured (unpredictable, high stress)
**Underserved group:** Immigrants unfamiliar with Norwegian healthcare system
**Description:** Immigrants from countries with different healthcare models are shocked by Norway's "fastlege" (GP) system: you can't just go to a specialist, you must be referred. Legevakt (emergency care) thresholds are perceived as very high. Medication that was over-the-counter in their home country requires prescription. Understanding "egenandel" (co-payment) and "frikort" (free card after threshold) is confusing. Ukrainian immigrants specifically express frustration and vulnerability with the system.
**Source:** forskning.no (2025), IMDi integration reports, Nordic Council (Nord2025-038)
**Why tech ignores them:** Language fragmentation (dozens of languages needed). Medical information carries liability. Small market per language group. Existing health portals (Helsenorge) assume Norwegian cultural context.
**Current solutions:** Helsenorge (Norwegian/English only, assumes system knowledge), IMDi information pages, municipality integration offices, volunteer interpreters. No interactive "healthcare navigation guide" exists for newcomers.
**Norwegian angle:** Directly Norwegian. The fastlege system, egenandel/frikort, and Helsenorge are uniquely Norwegian concepts that need explaining.

## Problem 15: Dugnad/Volunteer Shift Fairness Tracking
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Per dugnad season (monthly in active clubs)
**Underserved group:** Members of sports clubs, parent groups, housing cooperatives (borettslag)
**Description:** When a club organizes dugnads, the same 20% of members always show up while 80% find excuses. There's no transparent tracking of who contributed what. This breeds resentment and eventually burns out the active volunteers. Some clubs try "dugnadslister" but tracking is manual and confrontation-averse Norwegians hate calling people out. In borettslag (housing co-ops), dugnad participation is a persistent source of neighbor conflict.
**Source:** Idrettsforbundet dugnad guidelines, Frivillighet Norge, general association management literature
**Why tech ignores them:** Culturally sensitive (nobody wants to be the "dugnad police"). The "product" is essentially a shame ledger -- awkward to market. Tiny market per club. Spond partially addresses attendance but not fairness/contribution tracking.
**Current solutions:** Manual sign-up sheets, Spond events (attendance only), Facebook event RSVPs (unreliable). No tool tracks cumulative contribution across a season and presents it fairly.
**Norwegian angle:** Dugnad is as Norwegian as brown cheese. This is a uniquely Scandinavian cultural institution. Borettslag dugnad conflicts are a meme-level cultural phenomenon.

## Problem 16: Certification/License Expiry for Tradespeople
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Annually (but consequences of missing deadlines are severe)
**Underserved group:** Licensed tradespeople (electricians, plumbers, health workers)
**Description:** Tradespeople hold multiple certifications that expire at different times. Missing a renewal can mean inability to work, compliance violations, and losing insurance coverage. Most track expiry dates in their heads or on calendar reminders. One missed deadline can cascade into weeks of lost work and revenue.
**Source:** GetApp, Remindax, Safesite, TalentGuard, Virginia DPOR
**Why tech ignores them:** Too niche for general calendar apps, too simple for enterprise compliance software. Each trade/country has different certification requirements. Low revenue per user.
**Current solutions:** Calendar reminders, spreadsheets, enterprise tools like Remindax (overkill for solo operators). No simple "certification wallet" app for individual tradespeople.
**Norwegian angle:** Norwegian certifications (ADK1/ADK2 for refrigerants, electrical sertifikat, etc.) have specific renewal cycles. DSB (Direktoratet for samfunnssikkerhet) manages several trade registries.

## Problem 17: Borettslag/Sameie Communication & Decision Making
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Monthly (peaks around general assembly)
**Underserved group:** Board members and residents of Norwegian housing cooperatives
**Description:** Borettslag boards communicate via email, physical notices on bulletin boards, and increasingly through Facebook groups. But Facebook excludes non-users, email goes unread, and physical notices are ignored. Annual general assemblies (generalforsamling) require formal voting procedures that are managed with paper ballots or show of hands. Digital voting exists but is expensive. Neighbor disputes about everything from parking to noise to dugnad participation need a structured channel.
**Source:** General Norwegian housing cooperative management knowledge, borettslag law
**Why tech ignores them:** OBOS has its own portal but it's for OBOS-managed properties. Independent borettslag have no affordable digital platform. Market is uniquely Norwegian/Nordic.
**Current solutions:** OBOS portal (only for OBOS properties), Styreportalen (paid, targets larger sameier), email/Facebook, physical bulletin boards. Vibbo (residential community app) exists but is gaining traction slowly.
**Norwegian angle:** 100% Norwegian niche. ~350,000 borettslag units in Norway. Legal requirements for generalforsamling, regnskap, vedlikehold create specific digital needs.

## Problem 18: Parent Group (FAU) Coordination
**Pain level:** 6/10
**Frequency:** Weekly during school year
**Underserved group:** FAU (Foreldrenes arbeidsutvalg) members at Norwegian schools
**Description:** Every school has an FAU with elected parent representatives who organize events, coordinate volunteer efforts, manage class funds, and act as liaison between parents and school. They use WhatsApp groups (chaos), Spond (partly), email chains, and shared Google Docs. Every year the entire FAU rotates and all institutional knowledge disappears. No standardized handover process exists.
**Source:** General Norwegian school system knowledge, association management research
**Why tech ignores them:** Annual rotation means no customer loyalty. Zero budget. Each FAU is tiny (5-15 people). Too small to matter individually, collectively they represent every school in Norway.
**Current solutions:** WhatsApp/Facebook groups, Spond, Google Docs, school's own LMS (Visma InSchool/IST). None specifically designed for FAU workflows (event planning, fundraising, meeting minutes, handover).
**Norwegian angle:** Uniquely Norwegian institution. Every public school has a mandatory FAU. There's even a national FAU organization (FUG/KFU). Built-in distribution channel.

## Top 5 Recommendations for Hackathon MVP

### Tier 1: Best Combination of Pain + Buildability + Demo Impact

**1. Caregiver Family Coordination Hub (Problem #1 + #10)**
- Build: Daily check-in ("I visited mom, gave morning meds, she seems tired"), shared medication list, simple task assignment
- Why it wins: Universal emotional resonance, daily use case, clear demo narrative
- 5-day scope: Shared family dashboard + daily check-in form + medication list + notification

**2. Immigrant Settlement Navigator for Norway (Problem #4 + #14)**
- Build: Interactive checklist "Your first 90 days in Norway" covering UDI/NAV/kommune/BankID/fastlege/Lanekassen
- Why it wins: Clear problem, emotional pitch, judges can instantly understand the value
- 5-day scope: Step-by-step wizard with status tracking + healthcare explainer + available in multiple languages (start with English/Ukrainian/Arabic)

### Tier 2: Strong Contenders

**3. Small Club All-in-One (Problem #2 + #6 + #15)**
- Build: Member list + dues tracking (Vipps integration) + event/dugnad scheduling + simple financial overview
- Why it wins: Massive Norwegian market (50,000 clubs), relatable to judges
- 5-day scope: Member registry + dues tracker + dugnad sign-up with contribution history

**4. Student Housing Alert System (Problem #7)**
- Build: Aggregated alerts from Finn.no, Hybel.no, Facebook groups. Instant notification + scam detection + budget filter
- Why it wins: Timely (6,000 in queue), simple tech (scraping + notifications), relatable
- 5-day scope: Alert bot that monitors listings and sends filtered notifications

**5. Weather-Integrated Trade Scheduler (Problem #12)**
- Build: Calendar + Yr.no weather overlay + automatic client notification when weather forces rescheduling
- Why it wins: Novel concept, clear value prop, good demo ("rain tomorrow = automatic reschedule + client SMS")
- 5-day scope: Calendar with weather overlay + auto-notification templates

## Gotchas & Considerations

- **Medical/health apps** carry liability risk -- frame as "family communication tool" not "medical device"
- **Immigrant tools** need to avoid patronizing tone -- co-design with actual immigrants is ideal
- **Free tools** for volunteer orgs mean you need an alternative revenue model (freemium, sponsor, government grants)
- **Norwegian-specific** tools limit total addressable market but increase demo resonance for Norwegian judges
- **Elderly-focused** tools are hard to demo because the actual users aren't in the room
- **Dugnad tracking** could backfire culturally -- Norwegians resist surveillance of social obligations
- **Student housing** is fundamentally a supply problem -- an app helps with discovery but doesn't create housing
